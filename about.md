Before joining AISI, I worked at [Faculty](facultyai.com), the UK's leading applied AI consultancy. I built and validated models and software for solving military intelligence problems for demanding UK national security clients. My time at Faculty bookended the five years I spent working as a data scientist.

I started coding in 2011. I was introduced to ML by Andrew Ng's MOOC in 2016, which is roughly the time machine learning winter was thawing into its long and sweltering summer. I did a few research placements during my undergrad and wrote my bachelor's thesis on the robustness of deep reinforcement learning. Seeing that optimisation and statistics are the head and rear of the machine learning horse halloween costume, I did my Master's degree in mathematical statistics. I then tried out a PhD in statistics for a year.

The capability of AI systems has surged in the last decade. In 2014, software capable of classifying muddy images of simple objects was considered a state-of-the-art research result. Only a few well-resourced organisations integrated AI features into their products. The scope of these features was so narrow that it was described as "machine learning", since the connotations of "AI" were embarrassing. Most of these features were bells and whistles. The majority of research was publicly visible on Arxiv. Yes, even from commercial labs. 

In 2024, the AI landscape is different. Anyone with web access can use software which holds fluent conversation, generates photorealistic images, and understands novel problems in situ. AI systems have become a crux of commercial value for the most valuable companies in the world. Key details of the leading systems are secrets. The dirty term "AI" is now used by even the sternest academics, and they don't even blush.
