A fiction that everyone believes in is that of the self - that I am me, and you are you. It's a pervasive fiction: people fixate on their own selves and the selves of others. What do I want, how am I feeling, I love them, they have treated me badly, how can I improve, what do you want.

A strong signal of the monopoly of the self, besides how much time you spend thinking about yourself, is that it's almost always been chosen as the dominant unit of moral, political, and legal currency. It's an enormously common substructure of religions and ethical systems. Whether self-serving or altruistic, we're interested in which selves benefits and tolls accrue to, who owns what, and often especially what's in it for us. 

Because the self is a pervasive fiction, both in the sense that it's shared by everyone, and because of the proximity of "self" and "mind", it's easy to forget that it's a fiction. Meeting a person that does not have a self is rare. It's uncommon to sincerely consider, in principle at least, opting out of having one. 

The idea of choosing whether to have a self, deciding how tightly to embrace its fiction, is novel to me. I know and love that meme about us all being fingers of the universe and understand that non-self, whether as a choice or as an observation, is a major idea of Buddhism. And I regularly try not to be so selfish. But I've always interacted with non-self as a temporary therapeutic, rather than my default framework for thinking about things. Instead, my go-to way of seeing the world has been old reliable, the selfish agent-oriented view. 

The self touches all parts of life, so the effects of deciding not to have a self, or displacing it with an alternative concept, could be big. But, at first blush, it seems tricky to opt out. Even if you stop reasoning about yourself as being distinct from other things, other people are certainly going to do it. Most people model others as thinking and acting in terms of a perspective and motivations of a self, and so not having one may make it difficult to be legible to others i.e. people are going to assume you're cracked. That peer pressure will be persuasive. 

You've also lived your entire life asking "what should I do? what do I care about?". It may be uncomfortable or undesirable to set that aside. I found that writing this, I kept reflexively getting dragged into thinking about how non-self could enable aims of my self. 

Thinking in terms of a self makes a person see themselves as an agent. The self demarcs a locus of control and intention. We reason in terms of selves because we believe they are convenient units of control and action. The idea of agency relies on the idea of self: seeing ourselves as separate from others cues us to the possibility we can take action.

The self also represents an information boundary. People are privy to different information and selves allow us to talk about who has access to what information. Secrets and distrust of others arises because of selves. Information flow between selves is usually noisier than within a self and discoordination frequently become more likely when trying to coordinate across the boundary of the self. 

Selves put the self in selfish. The bedrock of competition in the social world is and has, for a long time, been the self: who gets what, and what's in it for me? By persuading a person that they have a self, you give them a key tool for selfish thinking. On the flipside, if you convince them that others have selves, you open a door to seeing the joy and pain they cause others, but you could acknowledge them as sentient without being a self. The self also makes an intoxicating promise to even the most altruistically-minded person: pursue self-serving actions instrumentally, so you can gain power to better help others.

Denying that some people have selves has been a historical go-to for making it permissible to do awful things to others. You can also try to avoid culpability by arguing that you don't have a self, or at least that a different self was responsible for your actions. If you have been ignored while waiting to order a drink at a bar, you know that you can find someone denying the existence of yourself upsetting.     

Although the self exists only in the mind, taking the idea of your self very seriously can torment people in the real world. Thoughts about ourselves seem more likely to cause physical pain than thoughts about others - unsatisfied desires, agonising decisions, impossible fantasies, wounding regrets. Not pain in the mind - pain in the body. By having a self, you create a big attack surface for psychological pain: you're suddenly able to transactionally analyse everything according to how it affects the mind that's in your body, and the ego that goes along with it. 

Thoughts about what you deserve and how you are treated can - and, I think frequently do - become addictive, counterproductive, and painful. They feel rubbish. And getting emotional from them can cause you to make poor decisions and hurt others. Focusing on your self too much also diminishes your self-awareness, blindness to the periphery that will also result in causing suffering. And! It incurs the opportunity cost of not reasoning in other units, such as an organisation or movement, which may be far more clarifying. 

You abandoning the idea of the self altogether, by yourself, seems futile and counter-productive. But it seems wise to consider how frequently thinking about yourself creates fertile ground for selfish and painful thoughts to sprout in, how selves are necessary for competition and distrust, and the consequences of accepting this fiction as an objective truth, rather than something that can be picked up and put down.
