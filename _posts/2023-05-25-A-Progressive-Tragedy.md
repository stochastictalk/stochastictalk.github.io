This post is designed for someone who is starting their career and takes their organization's progression and promotion frameworks very seriously. Maybe they spend a lot of time anxiously contemplating how they can rise meteorically through the ranks, or what the shortest path to a pay rise is. And perhaps they're also a bit suspicious of their blind appetite for power and money.

Many organizations have progression frameworks that are intended to orient the energies of their staff towards doing work that benefits the org (d'uh). This makes sense - an org's leadership has goals, and they want their staff to work towards those goals. So they create easy-to-use incentive frameworks that provide the as, bs, and cs in the alphabet of career advancement. By definition, people like incentives, so many staff structure their work (and by extension their career) around these progression frameworks.

Two problems you might encounter when trying to use your organization's progression framework:
* it might not at all reflect what you want from your career
* it also might not reflect what most other people (or the group that you care about) value

There is a massive number of things you could do with your career. Even within the same role title. A progression framework outlines only one or a handful of these things. Unfortunately, the incentive system for adhering to your organization's framework is really clear, and it's also readily accessible. No difficult contemplation required! This means the path of least resistance available to you is this: build your career in a way that is optimized for your organization, and not for you.

On top of this, the most remarkable and rewarding work, as measured by society, very likely isn't reflected by your organization's progression framework. All organizations create frameworks that fit their wants, and very few do what's best for all organizations. Consequently, organizations taken collectively suffer. This is the tragedy of the commons, for progression frameworks. 

So my suggestion is simple: take your progression framework way less seriously. Think for yourself about how you want to develop. Scrutinize the motvations underlying frameworks given to you, and seek out alternative resources to guide your development. 

Recognize that you can set the direction for your life yourself, and you don't need to enslave yourself to the progression frameworks and expectations you happen to encounter. It is not the path of least resistance, and the rewards aren't obvious, but the people I know who have applied this philosophy seem to be getting a better utility ROI than those who don't.
