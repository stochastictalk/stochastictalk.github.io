Midjourney, the San Francisco startup with broadly the best text-to-image models currently going, publishes a magazine featuring artwork generated by users of the company's models. I received my copy of Issue 2 last week. 

It's a wonderful magazine. Most pages contain one or two images, accompanied by the usernames of their progenitors and the prompts they used. It also contains an interview with a creative that has been using Midjourney.

![A photograph of Midjourney magazine.](assets/midjourney-magazine.webp)

I like art and enjoy the sorts of thoughts that it evokes, but I'm pretty ignorant of art history. This magazine signposted for me what artists consider worthy of depiction, and, better still, the words used to talk about different subject matter and styles. The prompts often spell out what I'm seeing. Here's an example: 

![A photograph of Slimesunday's artwork in Midjourney magazine.](assets/slimesunday.jpg)

And [Slimesunday's](https://slimesunday.com/) prompt: `depicting an imaginative scene of a crowd of people swimming inside a giant woman's mouth, as if it were a swimming pool party, people navigating around teeth, tongue, and other oral structures, playfully interacting with the unusual envionment in the style of coby whitmore, arthur sarnoff, and norman rockwell, professional color grading, clean sharp focus, hyperrealism theme Image Prompts --ar 4:5 --iw 2`

The prompt is displayed on the page opposite to the image, in crisp black sans serif font against a blank background, exactly as you'd find it in a museum label. Without the prompts, I would not have realised that this generation uses a colour palette likely to be found in an illustration or painting by Coby Whitmore or Norman Rockwell. Nor would I have known that its sharper detail is not found in paintings by those artists and is likely a consequence of being explicitly requested. I wouldn't even have known who Coby Whitmore was, or the connection between his career and the attributes of the woman with a mouth like a swimming pool.

The prompt is different to a museum label. It doesn't contain a curator's description of what I'm seeing, or even the artist's post hoc description of what they've created. Instead, it contains the artist's specification of what they wanted to create, establishing a significant linchpin in my interpretation of the image.

The availability of the artist's literal intention anchors me closer to the author's intention than I'd be when looking at a museum label, counter to the kind of many-worlds postmodern intepretative framework Roland Barthes promoted in [Death of the Author](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Death_of_the_Author). This interpretative positioning is opposite to the creative pose of text-to-image software and low-labour media synthesis: they could be seen as a postmodernist's dream, allowing an artist to explore thematic and stylistic landscapes helter-skelter.

To clarify, I don't think this software is a full realisation of that dream yet. The generations in the magazine are delightful, especially for being drawn from so many different genres of art and photography. But the same quality that makes them interesting collectively often makes them a bit more dull in isolation: how generic they are.

When I see several generations associated with similar prompts, it's much easier for me to discern stereotypical qualities of that genre or artist, which is quite cool. For instance, every one of the eight or so wildlife photographs in the magazine features exactly one animal centre-frame, in shallow depth of field, looking at out of the panel at the reader, typically with a vivid flair of colour in shot. It may be the magazine's editors were very fond of this style of wildlife photography, but my experience and understanding of these models leads me to think that users struggle to coerce the software and models into creating less generic content.

I've arrived at something of a truism: Midjourney, like any creative tool, is a gilded cage. The techniques underlying it and similar software are modern descendants of technology historically developed to describe things as they are, predictive models and algorithms for sampling from them. It is not surprising that the generations are not dazzlingly original. It's reductionist and inaccurate to suggest that all generative software offers is simply regurgitating minor variations on training data, however. However, now that photorealism is very nearly solved, I do find it more likely that the creative boundaries of these tools will be shifted most quickly through UI innovation rather than increasing model sophisitication (e.g. see tools like those being used to constantly extend the [Automatic1111](https://github.com/AUTOMATIC1111/stable-diffusion-webui) user interface).
